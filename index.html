Medicare Questions Answered

Medicare is a federal health insurance program for people age 65 and older, regardless of income or health status. It's also available to some younger people with disabilities and those with end-stage renal disease. Medicare has four parts: Part A (hospital insurance), Part B (medical insurance), Part D (prescription drug coverage), and optional Medigap or Medicare Advantage plans that provide additional coverage.
You become eligible for Medicare at age 65. Your Initial Enrollment Period (IEP) begins three months before your 65th birthday month and ends three months after. Enrolling on time is important because delaying without a qualifying exception can result in permanently higher premiums. You can also make changes during the Annual Enrollment Period (AEP), October 15 through December 7 each year, or during a Special Enrollment Period (SEP) if you qualify due to a life event such as moving or losing employer coverage.
Medicare Advantage (Part C) is an all-in-one alternative to Original Medicare offered by private insurers. It bundles Part A, Part B, and usually Part D prescription drug coverage into a single plan, often with added benefits like dental, vision, and hearing. These plans tend to have lower monthly premiums but may have network restrictions (HMO or PPO) and may require prior authorization for specific procedures. Medigap (Medicare Supplement) works alongside Original Medicare to cover out-of-pocket costs like copayments, coinsurance, and deductibles. Medicare Advantage enrollees also have the Open Enrollment Period (OEP), January 1 through March 31, to make one additional change. The Annual Enrollment Period (AEP) runs October 15 through December 7 each year. During AEP, anyone with Medicare can review their current coverage and make changes for the following year, effective January 1. This includes switching between Medicare Advantage and Original Medicare, changing Medicare Advantage plans, enrolling in or switching Part D prescription drug plans, and adding or dropping Medigap coverage (subject to underwriting outside your initial open enrollment). We recommend reviewing your plan every year because plan benefits, premiums, formularies, and provider networks can change annually.

Turning 65 in California: Your Complete Medicare Enrollment Guide

Published March 24, 2026 • Susana Marcos LLC, Licensed Medicare Insurance Agent • California

Turning 65 is a milestone, and in California it also marks the start of one of the most important health decisions of your life: enrolling in Medicare. Done right, you get excellent coverage without overpaying. Done wrong, you could face permanent financial penalties that follow you for the rest of your life.

This guide walks you through exactly when to enroll, what your options are, and how to get the most out of Medicare in California, including assistance programs many people do not know exist.

Why Age 65 Is the Trigger Date for Medicare

Medicare is the federal health insurance program for people 65 and older, as well as certain people under 65 with disabilities. Unlike employer coverage or Covered California plans, Medicare is not automatic for everyone. Most people need to actively sign up, and they have a limited window to do so without penalty.

If you currently have health coverage through your own active employment at a company with 20 or more employees, the rules are different and you may be able to delay Part B without penalty. But if you are retired, on COBRA, or covered only through a spouse's plan at a small employer (fewer than 20 employees), you need to enroll in Medicare at 65.

COBRA is not a delay exception. COBRA coverage does not count as active employer coverage for Medicare delay purposes. If you are on COBRA and turn 65, enroll in Medicare right away.

Your Enrollment Window: The Initial Enrollment Period (IEP)

You have a 7-month window to sign up for Medicare. Using the example of someone turning 65 in September 2026:

June 2026 (Month 1)

Your IEP opens 3 months before your birthday month. Enroll now for coverage starting September 1.

July 2026 (Month 2)

Coverage starts October 1 if you enroll this month.

August 2026 (Month 3)

Coverage starts November 1. Still no penalty, but you have a 2-month gap.

September 2026 (Birthday Month)

Coverage starts November 1 (same as August). Enrolling in or after your birthday month delays coverage further.

October, November, December 2026 (Months 5-7)

Still within IEP, no penalty yet, but coverage is delayed 2-3 months after enrollment.

After December 2026

Your IEP has closed. Late enrollment penalties begin. Part B adds 10% per 12-month period you were eligible but unenrolled.

The takeaway: enroll in the first 3 months of your IEP for the earliest coverage start and zero gap.

The Four Parts of Medicare: What You Need at 65

Part A: Hospital Insurance

Covers inpatient hospital stays, skilled nursing facility care, hospice, and some home health services. Part A is free for most people if they or their spouse worked and paid Medicare taxes for at least 40 quarters (10 years). If not, there is a premium up to $518/month in 2026.

Part B: Medical Insurance

Covers outpatient doctor visits, preventive care, lab tests, durable medical equipment, and some home health services. The standard Part B premium in 2026 is approximately $185/month, though it can be higher based on income (IRMAA adjustment). Unlike Part A, Part B always has a premium.

Part C: Medicare Advantage (optional replacement)

A private plan that replaces Original Medicare. Covered in detail below.

Part D: Prescription Drug Coverage

Not automatic. You must enroll in a standalone Part D plan or choose a Medicare Advantage plan that includes drug coverage (called MAPD). If you skip Part D when first eligible and do not have other creditable drug coverage, you will pay a permanent late enrollment penalty.

Choosing Between Original Medicare and Medicare Advantage

This is the biggest decision you will make at 65. Here is a side-by-side comparison:

Original Medicare (Parts A + B)

  • Administered by the federal government
  • No provider network: see any doctor or hospital that accepts Medicare nationwide
  • No annual out-of-pocket cap (you can add a Medigap supplement to cover the 20% you owe)
  • Does not include dental, vision, or hearing by default
  • More predictable costs if you add Medigap

Medicare Advantage (Part C)

  • Run by private insurers approved by Medicare
  • Provider network: HMO or PPO, must use in-network doctors
  • Annual out-of-pocket cap (protects against catastrophic costs)
  • Often includes dental, vision, hearing, and fitness benefits
  • Many plans in California have $0 additional premium

Neither option is universally better. The right choice depends on your health status, your doctors, your medications, and how often you travel or split time between states. Our guide on Medicare Advantage vs. Original Medicare in California goes deeper on this comparison.

California-Specific Assistance Programs

California has some of the most robust Medicare assistance programs in the country. If your income is limited, you may qualify for programs that dramatically reduce or eliminate your out-of-pocket costs:

Qualified Medicare Beneficiary (QMB)

Pays your Part A premium (if any), your Part B premium, deductibles, and cost-sharing. You essentially pay nothing for Medicare-covered services from providers who accept Medicaid.

Specified Low-Income Medicare Beneficiary (SLMB)

Pays your Part B premium only. A smaller benefit than QMB but still significant at roughly $185/month.

Extra Help (Low Income Subsidy for Part D)

Reduces or eliminates your Part D premium and drug copays. In 2026, full Extra Help can save beneficiaries $5,000 or more per year in drug costs.

Dual Eligibility: Medicare and Medi-Cal Together

If you qualify for both Medicare and Medi-Cal, you are considered dual-eligible. You may be able to enroll in a Dual Special Needs Plan (D-SNP), which coordinates both programs and in many cases eliminates nearly all out-of-pocket costs. Many D-SNPs in California also include additional benefits like transportation to medical appointments and over-the-counter allowances.

How to Enroll in Medicare in California

Enrolling in Parts A and B is done through Social Security, not through Medicare directly. You have three ways to apply:

  • Online: at ssa.gov (fastest option, available 24/7)
  • By phone: call Social Security at 1-800-772-1213 (TTY: 1-800-325-0778)
  • In person: at your local Social Security office (appointments recommended)

After enrolling in Parts A and B, you then choose your additional coverage: a standalone Part D plan, a Medigap plan, or a Medicare Advantage plan. What If I Am Still Working at 65?

If you work for an employer with 20 or more employees and have active employer-sponsored coverage, you can delay Part B without penalty. When you eventually retire or lose that coverage, you will have a Special Enrollment Period (SEP) of 8 months to enroll in Part B penalty-free. You will also have 2 months after losing employer drug coverage to enroll in Part D without penalty.

Be careful: once that 8-month SEP closes, you will need to wait for the General Enrollment Period (January 1 through March 31 each year), and your coverage would not begin until July of that year.

Frequently Asked Questions

When should I enroll in Medicare if I am turning 65 in California?

Enroll during the first 3 months of your Initial Enrollment Period (3 months before your birthday month) for the earliest coverage start and no coverage gap.

Is Medicare free for people turning 65 in California?

Part A is free for most people. Part B has a standard monthly premium of approximately $185 in 2026. California assistance programs like QMB and SLMB can pay these premiums for qualifying beneficiaries.

What is the difference between Medicare Advantage and Original Medicare when turning 65?

Original Medicare lets you see any Medicare-accepting provider nationwide with no out-of-pocket cap (you can add Medigap). Medicare Advantage is a private plan with a network, an annual out-of-pocket cap, and often includes extra benefits like dental and vision.

What is Medi-Cal and how does it relate to Medicare in California?

Medi-Cal is California's Medicaid program. Dual-eligible beneficiaries (both Medicare and Medi-Cal) can enroll in D-SNP plans that coordinate both coverages and may eliminate most or all out-of-pocket costs.

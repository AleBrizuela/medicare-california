How to Prepare for Medicare When You Turn 65 in California: Step-by-Step Guide

March 24, 2026 · 10 min read

Turning 65 is a milestone, and not just because of the celebrations. It is the age when most people become eligible for Medicare, the federal health insurance program. But enrolling is not as simple as it sounds. There are specific deadlines, multiple plan options, and costly penalties if you do not act on time. This guide explains exactly what to do, when to do it, and how to choose the right plan in California.

When Can I Enroll in Medicare?

Your first chance to enroll is called the Initial Enrollment Period (IEP). This window lasts 7 months:

  • 3 months before the month you turn 65
  • The month you turn 65
  • 3 months after the month you turn 65

Example: If you turn 65 in July 2026, your IEP runs from April 1 through October 31, 2026.

The best time to enroll is during the first 3 months (before your birthday). That way, your coverage starts on the first day of your birthday month. If you wait until after your birthday, there may be a one- to three-month delay before your coverage begins.

What Parts of Medicare Do I Need?

Medicare has several parts, and understanding each one is key to making the right decision.

Part A: Hospital Coverage

Covers hospital stays, skilled nursing facilities, hospice care, and certain home health services. Most people pay no premium for Part A if they worked and paid Medicare taxes for at least 10 years (40 quarters).

Part B: Medical Coverage

Covers doctor visits, outpatient services, medical equipment, and preventive services. The standard Part B premium in 2026 is $185.00 per month (it may be higher if your income is above certain thresholds).

Part C: Medicare Advantage

Medicare Advantage plans are offered by private insurers approved by Medicare. They replace Original Medicare (Parts A and B) and typically include prescription drug coverage, dental, vision, and hearing. Many plans in California have $0 premiums.

Part D: Prescription Drug Coverage

If you choose Original Medicare instead of Medicare Advantage, you will need a separate Part D plan to cover your prescription medications.

Three Paths for Your Medicare Coverage in California

When you turn 65, you have three main options:

Option 1: Medicare Advantage (Part C)

This option combines hospital, doctor, and usually drug coverage into a single plan. It is popular in California because many plans offer extras like dental, vision, hearing, medical transportation, and wellness programs, all with low or $0 premiums.

Best for: People who want comprehensive coverage with low monthly costs and are comfortable using a provider network.

Option 2: Original Medicare + Medigap + Part D

Original Medicare gives you the freedom to see any provider that accepts Medicare, without referrals. A Medigap (supplemental) plan covers the costs that Original Medicare does not pay, such as copays, coinsurance, and deductibles. You will need to add a Part D plan for prescriptions.

Best for: People who travel frequently, want to choose any doctor or specialist, and prefer predictable costs.

Important California fact: If you enroll in a Medigap plan within the first 6 months after your Part B becomes active, insurers must accept you regardless of your medical history. This guaranteed issue right is valuable and does not repeat.

Option 3: Delay Medicare (if still working)

If you are 65 but still working and have health insurance through an employer with 20 or more employees, you can delay Part B enrollment without penalty. Many people sign up for Part A (which is free) and delay Part B until they stop working.

Important: When you leave your job or lose employer coverage, you have an 8-month Special Enrollment Period (SEP) to enroll in Medicare without penalty.

Late Enrollment Penalties: What You Need to Know

If you do not enroll when you should and do not have qualifying employer coverage, you may face permanent penalties.

Part B Penalty

A 10% surcharge on your monthly Part B premium for each full 12-month period you were eligible but not enrolled. This penalty is added to your premium for life.

Part D Penalty

If you do not enroll in a prescription drug plan when you are first eligible and do not have other creditable coverage, you will pay an extra 1% for each month of delay, added to your plan premium. This is also permanent.

The best way to avoid penalties is to enroll during your IEP or make sure your employer coverage qualifies as "creditable coverage."

Step by Step: How to Enroll in Medicare in California

Step 1: Gather your documents (4 to 6 months before turning 65)

You will need your Social Security number, proof of citizenship or legal residency, and information about your current insurance.

Step 2: Enroll in Parts A and B (3 months before turning 65)

You can do this online at ssa.gov, by phone by calling Social Security, or in person at a local Social Security office.

Step 3: Decide your coverage path

Choose between Medicare Advantage or Original Medicare + Medigap + Part D. A licensed agent at Beneficios Medicare can compare options in your zip code at no cost.

Step 4: Enroll in your chosen plan

If you choose Medicare Advantage or a Part D plan, you can enroll during your IEP. If you choose Medigap, apply within the first 6 months after your Part B activates to guarantee acceptance.

Step 5: Confirm your coverage

Review your Medicare card and plan documents. Make sure your doctors are in network and your medications are covered before your first visit.

Common Mistakes When Enrolling in Medicare

  • Waiting too long: Missing your IEP can cause coverage delays and permanent penalties.
  • Assuming Social Security enrolls you automatically: If you are already receiving Social Security benefits, enrollment in Parts A and B may be automatic. But if you are still working and not receiving benefits, you need to sign up yourself.
  • Not comparing plans: There are dozens of Medicare Advantage and Medigap plans available in every California county. What works for a neighbor may not work for you.
  • Forgetting Part D: If you choose Original Medicare and do not add a Part D plan, you risk a permanent penalty and no drug coverage.
  • Not checking the provider network: Before enrolling in any plan, confirm that your preferred doctors and hospitals are included. For a detailed English-language guide on this topic, visit our turning 65 guide on Medicare California.

Frequently Asked Questions

When should I enroll in Medicare if I'm turning 65?

Your Initial Enrollment Period starts 3 months before your 65th birthday month and ends 3 months after. Ideally, enroll in the first 3 months so your coverage begins on the first day of your birthday month.

What happens if I don't enroll on time?

If you miss your IEP and do not have qualifying employer coverage, you face a 10% Part B premium penalty for each year of delay. This penalty is permanent and added to your monthly premium.

Can I delay Medicare if I'm still working?

Yes. If you have health coverage from an employer with 20 or more employees, you can delay Part B without penalty. When you lose that coverage, you have 8 months to enroll.

What is the difference between Medicare Advantage and Medigap?

Medicare Advantage replaces Original Medicare and bundles everything into one plan (hospital, doctor, drugs, and extras). Medigap supplements Original Medicare by covering the costs Medicare does not pay. You cannot have both at the same time.
